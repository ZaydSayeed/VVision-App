01 · THE FEEDBACK
02 / 10FROM THE ADVISOR SYNC
He didn't say we can't validate. He said to prove it first.

Six things that should change the plan.

01 · Prove before raising

Don't raise for 8 months. Validate utility across real dementia patients in multiple states first.

02 · The patient can't pay

Dementia patients on fixed income (~$1,800/mo) can't afford $499 + $40/mo. That demographic is the wrong buyer.

03 · Hardware is risky

Foundation is unproven. Hardware capital is expensive. VCs won't write meaningful checks without validation.

04 · Meta is coming

Ray-Ban Meta ships similar features. Could own the dementia software layer on their platform — or fight a losing hardware war.

05 · Insurance is a trap

CPT code process: 13–18 months, up to $1M. For now we can only market as General Wellness — not medical device.

06 · Adult children, not patients

The real customer may be the adult children of dementia patients. DTC to that cohort could make more money than selling to patients directly.

03 · PATHS WE TESTED
04 / 10THREE ANGLES · THREE VERDICTS
What we pressure-tested before choosing a direction

Three paths we rejected — and why.

Path A

Keep selling glasses to patients.

  • TAM Ayman cited ($50–80B) is total care spend. Real consumer SAM is $800M–$2B.
  • Patient-DTC has zero profitable comp. Every successful dementia-tech co. has an intermediary payer.
  • $300K+ needed to do hardware properly. Mismatched with our capital.
VIABLE · MISFRAMED
Path B

Software only on Meta's glasses.

  • Ray-Ban Meta has no third-party SDK. No dev program.
  • Meta offers no BAA — makes HIPAA compliance impossible on their hardware.
  • Raw camera access sandboxed. Face recognition may be physically blocked.
  • Meta's Orion demos already showed face ID — they eat verticals.
LIKELY A TRAP
Path C

$40 glasses. Sell the data.

  • BOM floor: $55–85. Above the proposed $70 retail price.
  • Anonymized behavioral data wholesale: $1–3/user/mo. Model needs 10–50× more.
  • BIPA fined Facebook $650M. Texas CUBI fined Meta $1.4B. One class action ends us.
  • Every consumer camera wearable under $200 has failed. See next slide.
FUNDAMENTALLY BROKEN

06 · WHAT CHANGES
07 / 10BEFORE · AFTER
One small change. Every constraint flips.

Same team. Same codebase. Different buyer.

× BEFORE — PATIENT-FIRST
The patient wears the glasses and learns the app.
  • Primary user: person with cognitive decline
  • Biggest unknown: will they tolerate wearable tech
  • Buyer ≠ user. Daughter buys, Dad refuses to wear.
  • Hardware-first. $300K+ to do properly.
  • HIPAA blocker on day one
✓ AFTER — CAREGIVER-FIRST
The adult child uses the app to care for their parent.
  • Primary user: adult child, 45–65, tech-literate, has income
  • Buyer = user = decision-maker. Same person.
  • Software-only launch. Hardware as V2 upsell.
  • Can start selling in <3 months
  • Non-PHI features ship first. HIPAA layers in.

The caregiver app is already the stronger half of our codebase. We've been building this product all along — we just led with the glasses.

08 · DATA STRATEGY
09 / 10THE LINE WE WILL NOT CROSS
Two tiers — one decided, one I want your take on

What we sell. What's on the table.

✓Clear path · sell with consent

  • Medication adherence patterns over time
  • Task completion and routine stability
  • Caregiver-reported symptom progression
  • Sleep and activity rhythms (aggregate)
  • Cognitive-decline trajectory markers

De-identified via HIPAA Safe Harbor. Opt-in, paid per cohort. Buyer: pharma + CROs. Real comps: Evidation ($153M), Flatiron ($1.9B exit), Tempus (~$8B).

?On the table · want your opinion

  • Facial recognition vectors (with consent)
  • Photos of recognized people
  • Raw camera frames from the glasses
  • Real-time location traces

Upside: potentially far higher value per user than behavioral data — pharma, insurers, AI training buyers.
Risk: classified as biometric under Illinois BIPA ($650M Meta settlement) and Texas CUBI ($1.4B Meta fine). Even with consent, consent-flow defects = class actions. Do we think the upside justifies building the compliance infrastructure, or do we keep all of this on-device?
